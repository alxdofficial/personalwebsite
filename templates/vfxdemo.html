Filming, Camera Tracking
Before any visual effects work can be done, the footage must first be tracked, or the motion of the camera must be estimated. High contrast features are needed for tracking, like the corner of a table, a spot in the wall, or a dedicated tracking marker. To a computer, two groups of pixels are the same feature if one is a translation or scale of the other within a HSL threshold. After the same feature is identified across multiple frames, it becomes a valid tracking point. The computer can then triangulate the camera's movement in 3D space using the 2D movement of many tracking points.
Rotoscoping
Sometimes it’s easier not to work with a green screen, but manually demarcating the foreground from the background can be a personal slice of hell, 60 times a second. Luckily, I have already switched most of my rotoscoping to an AI based solution. Runway ML and Davinci Resolve object mask do a decent job in detecting human and animal characters most of the time. When they don’t function the way I need to, I use Boris FX Mocha Pro to manually rotoscope. This is also the step to remove tracking markers using inpainting, as well as any unwanted elements in the footage.
Asset Creation, Layout
Everything that ends up on screen needs to be 3d modeled. There are a couple of ways to approach this: hard surface, sub-d, sculpting,procedural generation, kit-bashing, and outsourcing the process all together. I come from a background of sub-d modeling due to experience in automotive modeling, and it allows for flexibility in managing the poly count. However, maintaining quad topology needs to always be on the back of my mind, since it allows for stable animation rigging, shading, and integration of modifiers. Another method I recently grew fond of is geometry nodes (blender). It is highly valuable for instancing geometry on points distributed on another mesh. This is also the perfect tool to create abstract shapes and animations, as seen in the intro animation of this website.
Shading and Rendering
Shading is about modifying the interaction of light on meshes, not just adding color. The look and feel of any material in real life can be defined by normals, roughness, specularity, transmission, and emission. Normals alter the incident angle and direction of a lightray hitting the surface of a mesh, and give the illusion of additional detail where the geometry is actually flat. The roughness and specular fields describe the glossiness of a material. A lower roughness allows an object to have glass-like reflections of another shader, while a high roughness makes reflections from one source appear to come from every angle. Specularity applies more to emission sources, lamps, and it defines whether the wavelength of an emission source changes upon impacting a mesh. This used to be daunting when I started 3D software in the second half of highschool, but luckily this overview of shaders applies to all major render engines in many different softwares.
Composite, Edit, and Color
The rendered image from a 3d software is usually intentionally kept flat, as I may not have decided on the final look of the project. Using high bit depth EXR packaged frames, I get as much color latitude as grading log footage. This allows for flexible color correction to match the environment of live action footage, and later creative color grading without artifacts. I start every color grade with a color scheme, and the truth is most films use a variation of orange/yellow and teal/blue. The next thing I worry about is placing skin tones on the vectorscope line, while making it believable in relation to other lights in the scene. Finally, I add image imperfections like hallation, glow, and blur to bring my image closer to film.
